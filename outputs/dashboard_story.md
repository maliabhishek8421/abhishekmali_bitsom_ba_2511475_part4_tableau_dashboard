# Dashboard Story: Retail Performance Overview, FY2024–2025

## Executive Summary

Over the past two years the business generated ₹217.0M in sales and ₹33.3M in profit — a healthy 15.4% overall margin — across 4,200 orders. The business is stable, not declining and not rapidly growing, sitting at roughly ₹9M in sales per month with normal seasonal variation. The dashboard tells one consistent story when its views are read together: **Technology is the profit engine, Furniture is the drag, discounting beyond 30% is actively destructive to margin, and Furniture's high return rate is the same problem showing up twice — once in profitability, once in customer experience.**

## What Is Performing Well

Technology is carrying the business. It generates ₹154.0M of the ₹217.0M in total sales (71%) at an 18.2% margin — well above the company average — and every single Technology sub-category (Phones, Accessories, Copiers, Machines) sits above 18% margin individually. Return rates in Technology are also the lowest of the three categories at 3.0%. Regionally, no part of the country is a problem: even the lowest-margin region (West, 15.1%) is within half a point of the highest (East, 15.6%). Looking at the regional and category views side by side, the conclusion is the same in every region — Technology outperforms, and that pattern holds regardless of geography, which means it's a category-level strength, not a regional fluke.

## What Is Underperforming

Furniture is the clear underperformer, and it shows up the same way across three different views — category profitability, return analysis, and the discount-vs-profit relationship. Furniture sits at a 6.9% margin, less than half of Office Supplies (14.9%) and well under a third of Technology (18.2%), despite contributing nearly a quarter of total sales (23.8%). The sub-category view sharpens this further: Tables and Bookcases are the two weakest performers in the *entire* dataset at 5.7% margin each. Cross-referencing with the return analysis view shows this isn't a coincidence — Furniture also has the highest return rate of any category (7.7%, more than double Technology's 3.0%), and within Furniture, Bookcases, Furnishings, and Chairs are the three highest-return sub-categories company-wide. The same category is weak on profit *and* weak on customer experience — that combination, visible only by connecting the category and return views, is the dashboard's single most important finding.

## What Risks Are Visible

Two risks stand out when the discount-vs-profit view is read alongside the category view. First, discounting has a clear, almost linear cost: margin falls from 20.6% at 0% discount down to a **negative** 5.0% margin once discounts exceed 30% — meaning a meaningful slice of orders (64 orders, ₹2.07M in sales) are currently sold at a loss. Second, because Furniture already runs the thinnest margin of any category, it has the least room to absorb deep discounting before tipping into a loss — so the discounting risk and the Furniture-margin risk are not independent; they compound each other. Any analysis or policy on discount caps should look at Furniture first.

## What Opportunities Are Visible

The regional view shows real headroom: East and West regions match South's profit margin almost exactly (East 15.55% vs. South's 15.44%) while running at roughly 75–76% of South's order volume. That means the unit economics already work in those regions — the opportunity is volume, not pricing or cost structure, which is a more straightforward problem to solve (marketing reach, store/warehouse coverage, customer acquisition) than a margin problem would be. Similarly, since Technology already proves an 18%+ margin is achievable at scale, there's a clear benchmark to aim Furniture toward, even if it never fully closes the gap.

## Recommended Business Actions

1. **Prioritize a Furniture root-cause review** — pricing, supplier cost, and return reasons specifically for Tables, Bookcases, Chairs, and Furnishings — since this single category, read across the profitability and return views together, is the dashboard's biggest actionable problem.
2. **Cap or gate discounts above 30%** — the discount-vs-profit view shows this threshold is where orders flip to a net loss; this is a policy fix, not an investigation.
3. **Invest in East/West volume growth** — the regional view shows the margin risk is already solved there; the opportunity is purely about driving more orders.
4. **Monitor Home Office segment returns** — the segment view shows a 5.7% return rate vs. ~4% for the other two segments, worth a closer look even though sales and margin are healthy.

## Limitations of the Dashboard

- The dataset doesn't break out *why* an order was returned, so the Furniture/Home Office return findings point to *where* to investigate, not the root cause itself.
- Two years of history shows a seasonal dip pattern but isn't enough to confirm it repeats in year three.
- Region/state reflects shipping destination, not necessarily where the customer decision was made (e.g., corporate purchasing hubs) — worth confirming with the sales/ops team before acting on regional insights.
- The dashboard shows correlation (e.g., discount vs. margin, delivery delay vs. returns) — it does not prove causation, and shipping delay in particular shows no clear relationship with returns, which argues against assuming faster shipping would reduce returns.

## Suggested Next Analysis

- A cohort or order-level deep dive into the 64 orders with 31%+ discount, to see if they share a common cause (a specific sales rep, channel, or end-of-quarter timing).
- A product-level (not just sub-category) margin and return breakdown within Furniture to find the specific SKUs driving the weak numbers.
- A customer-level repeat-purchase and lifetime-value analysis for the Home Office segment, to understand whether the higher return rate is concentrated among a few customers or spread broadly.
